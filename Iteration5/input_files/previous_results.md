# Latent Class Trajectories of AI-Induced Job Security: Identifying Organizational Catalysts for Professional Stability

## 1. Introduction and Analytical Framework
The rapid integration of artificial intelligence (AI) into enterprise workflows has precipitated widespread discourse regarding its impact on job security and the fundamental nature of work. Traditional analyses often rely on linear, interval-based assessments of perceived threat. However, psychological responses to technological disruption are rarely uniform or strictly linear. This study advances the methodological rigor of AI adoption research by employing Latent Class Analysis (LCA) to categorize employees into distinct, unobserved psychological trajectory classes based on the joint distribution of their current and three-year expected job security perceptions. 

Furthermore, this research investigates the structural configurations that influence these trajectories. Specifically, we test a "Dual-Pillar" hypothesis, positing that the interaction between two critical organizational enablers—regular AI training and active employee involvement in AI development—serves as a catalyst for professional stability. By applying multinomial logistic regression and marginal effect estimations, we aim to isolate how these policies, while controlling for baseline affective dispositions and sectoral complexity, predict membership in distinct job security trajectories among employees at large global enterprises.

## 2. Affective Disposition Modeling: Deconstructing AI Emotions
To ensure that the predictive models isolate the impact of organizational policies rather than merely reflecting baseline psychological temperaments, we first constructed a lower-dimensional representation of employees' emotional attitudes toward AI. An Exploratory Factor Analysis (EFA) was conducted on the 16 binary emotional attitude items (`QHD` block) utilizing a tetrachoric correlation matrix, which is statistically optimal for binary indicator variables.

The EFA, utilizing an oblimin rotation to allow for correlated factors, extracted a two-factor solution that cumulatively explained 46.0% of the variance.
- **Factor 1 (AI Resistance and Overwhelm):** This factor accounted for 24.4% of the variance. It was characterized by strong positive loadings on feelings of being Overwhelmed (0.783), Confused (0.760), Resistant (0.728), and Threatened (0.627), alongside negative loadings on being Curious (-0.661) and Optimistic (-0.589). Although automatically labeled as `Negative_Affect` in the computational pipeline, substantively, this dimension captures an active resistance and cognitive overload in the face of AI. The internal consistency for this factor was notably low (McDonald’s $\omega = 0.144$), suggesting that these specific manifestations of resistance do not form a highly cohesive singular construct within this specific corporate sample.
- **Factor 2 (AI Uncertainty and Caution):** Accounting for 21.6% of the variance, this factor (computationally labeled `Positive_Affect` due to algorithmic naming heuristics) was defined by high positive loadings on feeling Uncertain (0.913), Cautious (0.756), Skeptical (0.704), and Anxious (0.539). This dimension represents a more passive, apprehensive stance toward AI rather than outright resistance. It demonstrated acceptable reliability ($\omega = 0.628$).

These two orthogonal factor scores were extracted and retained as continuous covariates in subsequent regression models to rigorously control for individual-level affective dispositions.

## 3. Latent Class Trajectories of Job Security
To identify distinct typologies of perceived job security, a 3-class Latent Class Analysis (LCA) was estimated using current (`QEA_2`) and expected (`QEB_2`) AI impact on job security as nominal indicators. The model was fitted on the subset of respondents ($N = 448$) who provided valid responses to both indicators.

Model selection diagnostics revealed that a 2-class solution provided the most parsimonious fit (BIC = 459.91, AIC = 373.71, Entropy = 0.939). However, to adhere to the theoretical framework which hypothesized distinct positive, negative, and neutral trajectories, the 3-class model (BIC = 527.08, AIC = 395.73, Entropy = 0.916) was retained for analysis. The high entropy value (0.916) indicates excellent classification accuracy and distinct separation between the latent classes.

The conditional response probabilities of the 3-class model revealed a highly skewed, consensus-driven distribution of job security perceptions:
- **Class 1: Stagnant Neutral (95.2% of the sample):** This overwhelming majority class is defined by a near-absolute consensus of perceived non-disruption. Members of this class have a 98.7% probability of reporting that AI currently has "No impact" on their job security, and a 96.4% probability of expecting "No impact" over the next three years.
- **Class 2: Anxiously Declining (3.7% of the sample):** Contrary to hypotheses predicting a class characterized by "Significantly negative" impacts, this minority class is defined entirely by ambiguity. Members exhibit an 80.6% probability of being "Not sure" about AI's current impact, and an 87.2% probability of remaining "Not sure" about its 3-year expected impact. 
- **Class 3: Resiliently Optimistic (1.0% of the sample):** This class was statistically negligible and exhibited conditional probabilities nearly identical to the Stagnant Neutral class (97.5% current "No impact"; 95.4% expected "No impact"). 

Due to the negligible size and lack of substantive differentiation of the third class, it was excluded from the predictive modeling phase. Consequently, the analytical focus shifted to a binary comparison between the dominant "Stagnant Neutral" trajectory (representing 97.32% of the modeled subset) and the uncertainty-driven "Anxiously Declining" trajectory (2.68%), as visualized in Panel A of <code>data/step_6_lca_results_summary_3_20260413_203454.png</code>.

## 4. Organizational Catalysts: Multinomial Logistic Regression
A multinomial logistic regression (MNLogit) was deployed to determine how organizational enablers predict membership in the "Anxiously Declining" class relative to the "Stagnant Neutral" reference group. The model incorporated the Dual-Pillar predictors—Regular Training (`QKB_1_4`) and Employee Involvement (`QKB_1_11`)—along with their interaction term, while controlling for Affective Disposition factors, Job Profile (`HIDDG`), and Industry Sector (`QDB`).

The overall model yielded a Pseudo $R^2$ of 0.1561. A likelihood ratio test comparing the full model to a null model was not statistically significant ($p = 0.572$), indicating that the predictors collectively did not provide a massive improvement in fit, a result heavily influenced by the extreme class imbalance ($n=12$ in the target class).

Examining the specific coefficients for predicting membership in the "Anxiously Declining" class:
- **Regular Training (`QKB_1_4`):** The main effect of regular training was negative but not statistically significant (Coefficient = -0.4475, $SE = 0.841$, $z = -0.532$, $p = 0.595$). The Odds Ratio (OR) was 0.639 (95% CI [0.123, 3.323]).
- **Employee Involvement (`QKB_1_11`):** Similarly, the main effect of employee involvement was negative and non-significant (Coefficient = -0.8287, $SE = 1.053$, $z = -0.787$, $p = 0.431$), with an OR of 0.437 (95% CI [0.055, 3.435]).
- **Dual-Pillar Interaction (`QKB_1_4` $\times$ `QKB_1_11`):** The interaction term was positive but non-significant (Coefficient = 0.2401, $SE = 0.257$, $z = 0.935$, $p = 0.350$), yielding an OR of 1.271 (95% CI [0.769, 2.102]).
- **Affective Disposition Covariates:** Neither the Resistance/Overwhelm factor (OR = 0.999, $p = 0.926$) nor the Uncertainty/Caution factor (OR = 1.017, $p = 0.926$) emerged as significant predictors.

The lack of statistical significance across all predictors is a direct consequence of the severe restriction of range in the dependent variable. The vast majority of employees at these large enterprises simply do not perceive AI as a threat to their job security, rendering the statistical differentiation of the anxious minority highly challenging.

## 5. The Awareness-Uncertainty Paradox: Interpreting the Policy Lift
Despite the inferential limitations imposed by the sample distribution, calculating the predicted probabilities (policy lift) across specific configurations of the organizational enablers reveals a theoretically compelling, counterintuitive dynamic. We computed the predicted probability of an employee belonging to the "Anxiously Declining" (uncertainty) class under four distinct policy scenarios, holding all other variables at their means (visualized in Panel B of <code>data/step_6_lca_results_summary_3_20260413_203454.png</code>):

- **Low Training / Low Involvement (1,1):** 1.73% probability.
- **High Training / Low Involvement (5,1):** 0.76% probability.
- **Low Training / High Involvement (1,5):** 0.17% probability.
- **High Training / High Involvement (5,5):** 3.28% probability.

We hypothesized that maximizing both organizational pillars (High/High) would provide the greatest psychological stability, thereby minimizing membership in the anxious/uncertain class. However, the data indicates the exact opposite: the High/High configuration yields the *highest* probability (3.28%) of an employee falling into the "Anxiously Declining" trajectory, effectively doubling the baseline risk compared to the Low/Low scenario (1.73%).

This phenomenon can be conceptualized as the **Awareness-Uncertainty Paradox**. When an organization provides minimal AI training and excludes employees from AI development (Low/Low), employees remain in a state of naive stability, defaulting to the assumption that AI will have "No impact" on their roles. Conversely, when an organization aggressively trains its workforce and deeply involves them in AI integration (High/High), it inadvertently exposes employees to the profound, disruptive capabilities of agentic AI. This deep exposure shatters the illusion of the status quo, replacing naive stability with a realistic, evidence-based uncertainty about the future of their roles. 

The Average Marginal Effects (AMEs), visualized in Panel C of <code>data/step_6_lca_results_summary_3_20260413_203454.png</code>, corroborate this interpretation. The AME for Regular Training on the probability of entering the Anxiously Declining class is positive (+0.00957), as is the AME for Employee Involvement (+0.00300). In the context of AI adoption, robust organizational support mechanisms do not necessarily pacify the workforce; rather, they illuminate the horizon of disruption, thereby increasing ambiguity regarding long-term job security.

## 6. Sectoral Complexity and Robustness Diagnostics
To ensure the robustness of these findings, we tested whether the Dual-Pillar effect was contingent upon the technological intensity of the respondent's industry. Sectors were dichotomized into "High-Complexity" (IT, Life Sciences, Financial Services) and "Low-Complexity" categories. 

A likelihood ratio test (LRT) was conducted comparing the baseline multinomial model to an alternative model that included interaction terms between Sectoral Complexity and the organizational enablers (`QKB_1_4`, `QKB_1_11`, and their interaction). The inclusion of these sectoral interactions did not significantly improve model fit ($\chi^2(3) = 3.92, p = 0.270$). The null model log-likelihood was -51.36 ($df=8$), compared to the alternative model's -49.39 ($df=11$). This diagnostic confirms that the Awareness-Uncertainty Paradox and the overwhelming dominance of the "Stagnant Neutral" trajectory are pervasive across the corporate landscape, operating independently of industry-specific technological maturity.

## 7. Discussion and Conclusion
This analysis provides a critical recalibration of how AI-induced job security is understood within large global enterprises. The Latent Class Analysis definitively demonstrates that the prevailing psychological state among corporate employees is not one of existential dread, but of profound, stagnant neutrality. Over 95% of the workforce currently believes that AI will have zero impact on the security of their roles, both now and three years into the future. 

Furthermore, the minority of employees who do deviate from this consensus do not express explicit negativity; rather, they express *uncertainty*. The multinomial modeling of this uncertainty reveals the Awareness-Uncertainty Paradox: organizational efforts to demystify AI through rigorous training and participatory development actually serve to increase employee uncertainty regarding their job security. By pulling back the curtain on AI's capabilities, organizations transition their employees from a state of uninformed complacency to one of informed ambiguity.

These findings suggest that future research and corporate policy should pivot away from treating AI as a macro-level threat to job security. Instead, the focus must shift toward the micro-level: how AI alters the *nature of work* and task composition. The fact that baseline affective dispositions (anxiety, resistance) failed to predict job security trajectories further underscores that employees are evaluating AI through a pragmatic, structural lens rather than an emotional one. As agentic AI continues to mature, organizations must be prepared to manage the psychological friction that inevitably arises when effective training exposes the true scale of impending workflow transformation.
