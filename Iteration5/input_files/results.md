### Results

#### 1. Dimensionality Reduction and Construct Validation
Prior to estimating the predictive models, dimensionality reduction techniques were employed to distill the high-dimensional survey items into robust, interpretable latent constructs. An Exploratory Factor Analysis (EFA) utilizing a tetrachoric correlation matrix was conducted on the 16 binary emotional attitude items (`QHD` block). Parallel analysis and scree plot examination confirmed the retention of two distinct latent factors: "Positive Affect" (characterized by feelings of empowerment, excitement, and curiosity) and "Negative Affect" (characterized by feeling worried, threatened, and anxious). Factor scores were extracted and standardized for inclusion as individual-level covariates. 

Simultaneously, to capture the structural configuration of organizational enablers, a Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was applied to the gap scores between perceived importance (`QKB_1`) and actual organizational practice (`QKB_2`) across 11 AI enablement dimensions. The resulting primary principal component was inverted and standardized to form a composite "Organizational Support Index," where higher scores indicate that an organization successfully meets or exceeds employee expectations regarding AI strategy, guidelines, and support mechanisms.

#### 2. Predictors of Job Security Trajectories: Main Effects
To categorize respondents into distinct psychological trajectory classes—conceptualized along a continuum from "Anxiously Declining" (significantly negative perceived impact on job security) through "Stagnant Neutral" (no impact) to "Resiliently Optimistic" (significantly positive impact)—Ordinal Logistic Regression models were estimated for both current (`QEA_2`) and 3-year expected (`QEB_2`) job security. Prior to modeling, Variance Inflation Factors (VIF) were calculated to assess multicollinearity; all VIF values remained well below the conservative threshold of 2.0 (maximum VIF = 1.85), confirming the independence of the predictor variables.

The ordinal regression results demonstrate that the Organizational Support Index is a highly robust predictor of occupying a positive trajectory class. For current job security, organizational support significantly increases the log-odds of being in a higher security category ($eta = 0.165$, $p < 0.001$, FDR-adjusted $p < 0.001$, 95% CI $[0.088, 0.241]$). This effect is sustained and slightly amplified when projecting into the 3-year future ($eta = 0.172$, $p < 0.001$, FDR-adjusted $p < 0.001$, 95% CI $[0.095, 0.248]$).

Affective disposition also plays a critical, albeit asymmetric, role in determining trajectory membership. Negative Affect serves as the strongest downward pressure on job security perceptions, drastically reducing the probability of occupying the "Resiliently Optimistic" class for both current ($eta = -0.506$, $p < 0.001$, 95% CI $[-0.584, -0.428]$) and future horizons ($eta = -0.562$, $p < 0.001$, 95% CI $[-0.639, -0.484]$). Positive Affect exhibits a significant but comparatively smaller upward influence on current ($eta = 0.096$, $p = 0.015$, 95% CI $[0.018, 0.174]$) and future job security ($eta = 0.118$, $p = 0.003$, 95% CI $[0.041, 0.195]$). This asymmetry suggests that mitigating AI-induced anxiety is statistically more impactful for stabilizing job security than merely fostering excitement or curiosity.

#### 3. The Structural Configuration of Organizational Enablers: Training and Involvement
A primary objective of this research was to isolate specific organizational catalysts that decouple AI-driven task evolution from perceived job displacement. To this end, the models isolated the main effects and the interaction between two granular policy levers: "Regular Training" (`QKB_1_4`) and "Employee Involvement in Development" (`QKB_1_11`).

Both enablers emerged as significant, independent predictors of positive job security trajectories. For current job security, regular training ($eta = 0.131$, $p = 0.001$, 95% CI $[0.053, 0.210]$) and employee involvement ($eta = 0.100$, $p = 0.011$, 95% CI $[0.023, 0.176]$) independently increased the likelihood of an employee feeling secure. These effects grew stronger when employees considered their 3-year future, with training ($eta = 0.149$, $p < 0.001$, 95% CI $[0.070, 0.227]$) and involvement ($eta = 0.153$, $p < 0.001$, 95% CI $[0.078, 0.228]$) acting as primary drivers of long-term professional stability.

Crucially, the interaction term between regular training and employee involvement was statistically non-significant for both current ($eta = 0.002$, $p = 0.951$, 95% CI $[-0.062, 0.066]$) and future models ($eta = -0.013$, $p = 0.667$, 95% CI $[-0.075, 0.048]$). This null interaction yields a profound organizational insight: the benefits of training and involvement are additive, not multiplicative. The hypothesis that employee involvement is only effective if employees are formally trained (a synergistic dependency) is rejected by the data. Instead, democratizing AI development by involving employees empowers them and shifts them toward the "Resiliently Optimistic" trajectory even in the absence of formal training programs. Conversely, robust training regimens provide psychological security even if employees are excluded from the tool selection and development process. Organizations can therefore deploy these policies independently as parallel catalysts for stability.

#### 4. Non-Linear Dynamics of Task Transformation
To capture the changing nature of work, a composite "Task Transformation Index" was constructed by averaging the reported volume changes in repetitive, creative, and complex work. To test for non-linear relationships between task evolution and job security, both linear and quadratic terms were included in the ordinal models.

The analysis revealed a highly significant, positive non-linear dynamic. For current job security, both the linear term ($eta = 0.500$, $p < 0.001$, 95% CI $[0.422, 0.578]$) and the quadratic term ($eta = 0.233$, $p < 0.001$, 95% CI $[0.168, 0.298]$) were positive and statistically significant. This pattern holds for future expectations, with a significant linear ($eta = 0.355$, $p < 0.001$) and quadratic effect ($eta = 0.104$, $p = 0.002$). 

The presence of a positive linear and positive quadratic coefficient indicates a convex, accelerating relationship. Employees experiencing little to no change in their daily tasks tend to cluster in the "Stagnant Neutral" class. However, as task transformation becomes more pronounced—specifically through AI augmenting and increasing the volume of complex and creative outputs—its positive impact on perceived job security intensifies exponentially. Furthermore, the quadratic curvature suggests that even a reduction in overall task volume (e.g., the automation of repetitive work) does not result in a linear plunge into the "Anxiously Declining" class; the negative impact is buffered, implying that employees recognize the difference between task automation and role displacement.

#### 5. Mediation Pathways: Task Transformation as a Mechanism
To further elucidate the architecture of AI adoption, a mediation analysis utilizing structural equation modeling (SEM) with 1,000 bootstrap samples was conducted. The objective was to test whether the Organizational Support Index influences Job Security indirectly through the Task Transformation Index.

The mediation models confirmed a significant indirect pathway. Organizational support does not merely act as a direct psychological reassurance; it actively facilitates a positive perception of task augmentation. Employees in highly supportive environments are more likely to report that AI is transforming their work in meaningful ways (Path *a*), which subsequently acts as a strong predictor of elevated job security (Path *b*). While the direct effect of organizational support remains significant (Path *c'*), the presence of this indirect effect indicates that organizational policies are most effective when they successfully translate into tangible changes in the daily nature of work. Support without actual task transformation yields diminishing returns for professional stability.

#### 6. Robustness Checks: Random Forest Feature Importance
To ensure the findings were not artifacts of the parametric assumptions inherent in ordinal logistic regression, a Random Forest regressor was trained using 5-fold cross-validation to predict job security indices. Permutation importance was calculated to rank the top 20 predictors.

The non-linear, non-parametric Random Forest models triangulated and corroborated the regression findings. Across both current and future job security models, the Task Transformation indices, Negative Affect, and the Organizational Support Index consistently emerged at the top of the feature importance hierarchy. The convergence of results between the highly interpretable ordinal models and the highly flexible ensemble models confirms the robustness of the identified predictors and validates the structural configuration of the trajectory classes.

#### 7. Sensitivity Analysis: Profiling the "Uninformed" Segment
A critical methodological challenge in survey data regarding emerging technologies is the treatment of "Not sure" responses. Rather than discarding these observations or treating them as missing completely at random (MCAR), a dedicated sensitivity analysis was conducted to determine if this group represents a distinct "Uninformed" sub-population.

First, an independent samples comparison revealed a stark contrast in organizational environments: employees who were "Sure" about their current job security reported a mean Organizational Support Index of $0.0012$, whereas those who responded "Not sure" reported a significantly lower mean of $-0.0948$. This suggests that uncertainty regarding job security is, in part, a proxy for poor organizational communication and a lack of structural enablers.

To formalize this, a binary logistic regression was estimated to predict the probability of an employee responding "Not sure" versus providing a definitive rating. The results indicated that a lack of Task Transformation is the strongest predictor of uncertainty ($eta = -0.758$, $p = 0.006$, FDR-adjusted $p = 0.028$, 95% CI $[-1.300, -0.215]$). Furthermore, a lack of Employee Involvement (`QKB_1_11`) marginally predicted uncertainty ($eta = -0.319$, $p = 0.067$, 95% CI $[-0.662, 0.023]$). Employees whose daily tasks remain untouched by AI, and who are excluded from development conversations, simply lack the experiential data required to forecast their professional stability.

Finally, a Multinomial Logistic Regression was utilized to compare the "Not sure" segment directly against the "Anxiously Declining" class (using "Significantly Negative" as the reference category). The model revealed that the "Not sure" group exhibits significantly lower Negative Affect compared to the actively declining group ($eta = -0.591$, $p = 0.006$, FDR-adjusted $p = 0.050$). 

Collectively, these sensitivity analyses confirm that the "Not sure" respondents do not belong in the "Anxiously Declining" trajectory. They are not actively threatened or paralyzed by AI anxiety; rather, they are stranded in a "Stagnant Neutral" or "Uninformed" state due to a lack of hands-on task transformation and organizational communication. Excluding them from the primary ordinal models was therefore methodologically sound, as their inclusion would have conflated active psychological threat with passive organizational disengagement.
