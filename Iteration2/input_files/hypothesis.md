The "Curse of Knowledge" paradox identified in Iteration 2 suggests that employee involvement in AI development is a double-edged sword: it increases perceived job security for some while heightening anxiety for others. I hypothesize that the moderating variable for this effect is the **"Alignment of AI Goals" (QED block)**. Specifically, I propose that employee involvement (`QKB_1_11`) only acts as a positive catalyst for job security when the company’s stated AI goals (`QED_1_1`–`QED_1_5`) prioritize "freeing up time for high-value work" or "reducing errors," whereas involvement in companies with goals focused on "reducing costs" or "completing tasks faster" (efficiency-only) triggers the "Anxiously Declining" trajectory. 

To test this, I propose a **Moderated Latent Class Analysis (MLCA)**. Instead of treating organizational enablers as a monolithic index, we will interact the `QKB_1_11` (Involvement) variable with a binary "Goal Orientation" index (Efficiency-driven vs. Augmentation-driven). This will determine if the "Curse of Knowledge" is a structural failure of the involvement process itself or a rational response by employees to the specific strategic intent behind the AI deployment. This approach moves beyond the "additive vs. multiplicative" debate by identifying the *strategic context* required for participatory management to function as a protective buffer rather than a source of threat.
