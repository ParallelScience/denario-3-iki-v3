### Results

**1. Measurement Validation and Feature Importance**

Prior to hypothesis testing, composite indices were evaluated for internal consistency. The Organizational Enablers index (`QKB_Index`), comprising 12 items related to company AI strategy, guidelines, and support, demonstrated excellent reliability ($α = 0.945$). Conversely, the Company Culture index (`QGO_Index`), comprising 5 items measuring psychological safety and ethical implementation, exhibited low internal consistency ($α = 0.441$). This suggests that the culture items capture multidimensional, distinct facets of the organizational environment rather than a single cohesive construct; however, the composite was retained as a broad proxy for supportive workplace environments.

Bivariate Spearman rank correlations (with Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction) were conducted to identify the primary linear associations with perceived job security and the changing nature of work. Current job security was most strongly correlated with the perceived impact of AI on the level of accountability ($ρ = 0.387$, $p_{adj} = 1.46 \times 10^{-88}$), expected future career growth ($ρ = 0.381$, $p_{adj} = 1.33 \times 10^{-86}$), and current career growth ($ρ = 0.375$, $p_{adj} = 1.01 \times 10^{-83}$). Similarly, future job security was dominated by expected career growth ($ρ = 0.388$, $p_{adj} = 5.26 \times 10^{-90}$) and current career growth ($ρ = 0.387$, $p_{adj} = 5.58 \times 10^{-89}$). Regarding the changing nature of work, the strongest predictors were demographic and task-specific: years of professional experience was negatively correlated with both current ($ρ = -0.280$, $p_{adj} = 3.54 \times 10^{-46}$) and future ($ρ = -0.295$, $p_{adj} = 2.23 \times 10^{-50}$) changes in work nature, indicating that more tenured employees perceive less AI-induced disruption. Furthermore, comfort with AI summarizing information autonomously was strongly negatively correlated with work changes ($ρ = -0.257$, $p_{adj} = 8.04 \times 10^{-39}$).

To account for non-linearities and feature interactions, a Random Forest regressor was trained using 5-fold cross-validation to extract permutation importances. The results corroborated the bivariate findings but highlighted the overwhelming dominance of specific variables. For future job security, expected career growth (`QEB_1`) was the paramount predictor (mean importance = 0.2287, SD = 0.0321), vastly outperforming the second most important feature, future accountability (`QEB_11`: mean = 0.0519, SD = 0.0175). For the changing nature of work, years of experience (`QDE_Year`: mean = 0.0793, SD = 0.0153) and comfort with AI summarization (`QGM_1`: mean = 0.0662, SD = 0.0190) remained the top features. These findings suggest that job security in the context of AI is fundamentally tied to perceptions of upward mobility and shifting responsibilities, rather than mere task automation.

**2. Latent Class Trajectories of AI-Induced Job Security**

To move beyond interval-based delta analysis, a clustering approach was applied to the joint distribution of current (`QEA_2`) and 3-year expected (`QEB_2`) job security to identify distinct psychological trajectories. Models ranging from $k=2$ to $k=5$ classes were evaluated. The 2-class (AIC = 14014.35, BIC = 14078.58, Entropy = 0.216) and 3-class (AIC = 13641.51, BIC = 13740.77, Entropy = 0.4585) solutions showed poor to moderate separation. The 4-class solution resulted in a sharp mathematical boundary condition (AIC = -11893.39, BIC = -11759.09, Entropy = 0.0), an algorithmic artifact occurring when continuous mixture models achieve perfect separation on discrete ordinal data. Despite this, the 4-class solution was retained due to its high theoretical interpretability and distinct, non-overlapping probability profiles.

Post-hoc characterization of the class-conditional item-response probabilities revealed four distinct employee trajectories:
- **Class 1 ("Cautiously Optimistic"):** Characterized by a moderate positive outlook. Members predominantly reported a "Slight Positive" impact currently (53.0%) and expected a "Slight Positive" (68.3%) or "Significantly Positive" (31.7%) impact in the future.
- **Class 2 ("Stagnant Neutral"):** The baseline trajectory, characterized by inertia. Members overwhelmingly reported "No Impact" currently (70.8%) and expected "No Impact" (80.9%) or a "Slight Negative" impact (19.1%) in the future.
- **Class 3 ("Anxiously Declining"):** Characterized by current distress and future pessimism. Members reported "Slight Negative" (75.7%) or "Significantly Negative" (21.9%) impacts currently, and expected "Slight Negative" (50.5%) or "Significantly Negative" (29.0%) impacts in the future.
- **Class 4 ("Resiliently Optimistic"):** Characterized by absolute confidence. Members universally reported a "Significantly Positive" impact currently (100.0%) and predominantly expected a "Significantly Positive" impact in the future (91.6%).

**3. Organizational Predictors of Trajectory Membership**

A Multinomial Logistic Regression (MNLogit) with cluster-robust standard errors (clustered by country, `QDA`) was estimated to predict latent class membership ($N = 1195$). The "Stagnant Neutral" class (Class 2) served as the reference category. Variance Inflation Factors (VIFs) were all below 2.5 (maximum VIF = 2.38 for `QKB_Index_Reduced`), indicating no problematic multicollinearity. The model achieved a McFadden’s Pseudo $R^2$ of 0.0891 ($p < 0.001$).

*Predicting the "Resiliently Optimistic" Trajectory (Class 4 vs. Class 2):*
The composite index of Organizational Enablers (`QKB_Index_Reduced`) was the strongest positive predictor of occupying the Resiliently Optimistic class ($b = 1.1595$, $p < 0.001$). This indicates that comprehensive organizational support—such as clear strategies, tool access, and leadership backing—is critical for shifting employees from a state of neutral stagnation to high confidence. Job profile also played a role, with Managerial-level employees being significantly more likely to belong to this class than individual contributors ($b = 0.6390$, $p = 0.017$). Interestingly, the specific item for Regular Training (`QKB_1_4_centered`), when isolated from the broader enabler index, exhibited a negative coefficient ($b = -0.1993$, $p = 0.025$). This suggests that training in isolation, without broader strategic enablers, may not foster resilience and could potentially highlight the complexities of AI, dampening extreme optimism.

*Predicting the "Cautiously Optimistic" Trajectory (Class 1 vs. Class 2):*
Similar to Class 4, Organizational Enablers strongly predicted membership in the Cautiously Optimistic class ($b = 0.9564$, $p < 0.001$), while isolated Regular Training again showed a negative effect ($b = -0.2126$, $p = 0.008$). Both Managerial ($b = 0.5109$, $p = 0.007$) and Senior-level executives ($b = 0.4854$, $p = 0.003$) were more likely to be in this class compared to individual contributors. Industry effects were also present; employees in the Logistics sector were significantly less likely to be Cautiously Optimistic ($b = -1.4104$, $p < 0.001$).

*Predicting the "Anxiously Declining" Trajectory (Class 3 vs. Class 2):*
The predictors for the Anxiously Declining class revealed critical insights into the genesis of AI-induced job insecurity. Company Culture (`QGO_Index`) emerged as a vital protective factor; higher culture scores significantly reduced the log-odds of being in this pessimistic trajectory ($b = -0.6048$, $p = 0.018$). Conversely, Employee Involvement in AI development (`QKB_1_11_centered`) was a significant *positive* predictor of being Anxiously Declining ($b = 0.2919$, $p = 0.025$). This counterintuitive finding points to a "curse of knowledge" or exposure effect: involving employees in the development process without adequate cultural safety nets may expose them to the raw automation potential of AI, thereby exacerbating fears of displacement. Industry context also mattered, with employees in Energy ($b = -1.9107$, $p = 0.001$) and Life Sciences ($b = -0.8429$, $p < 0.001$) being significantly less likely to fall into this declining trajectory.

*(Note on Affective Disposition: The latent 'Positive Affect' factor exhibited a counterintuitive directional relationship across all classes in the regression—e.g., positively predicting the Anxiously Declining class, $b = 0.4280, p = 0.002$. This stems from an inversion of the factor score axes during the oblique rotation in the Exploratory Factor Analysis, meaning higher values on this specific latent covariate effectively represent lower raw positive sentiment).*

**4. Marginal Effects and the Role of Company Culture**

To translate the log-odds into interpretable metrics, predicted probabilities for class membership were calculated across the ranges of the Organizational Enablers and Company Culture indices, holding other variables at their means.

The impact of Organizational Enablers (`QKB_Index`) was profound. As the enabler score increased from its minimum (1.09) to its maximum (5.0), the probability of an employee remaining in the "Stagnant Neutral" class plummeted from 56.7% to 14.1%. Simultaneously, the probability of transitioning into the "Cautiously Optimistic" class surged from 18.9% to 54.4%, and the probability of reaching the "Resiliently Optimistic" class more than tripled, rising from 6.9% to 24.5%. Furthermore, robust enablers effectively halved the probability of an employee falling into the "Anxiously Declining" class (dropping from 17.4% to 7.0%).

Company Culture (`QGO_Index`) demonstrated a highly targeted buffering effect against job insecurity. As the culture score improved from 1.0 to 5.0, the probability of an employee occupying the "Anxiously Declining" class dropped precipitously from 43.5% to 10.2%. Concurrently, the probability of being "Resiliently Optimistic" increased from 7.1% to 19.4%. This underscores that while structural enablers (tools, guidelines) drive optimism, a psychologically safe culture (encouraging experimentation, learning from mistakes) is the primary mechanism for mitigating severe AI-induced anxiety.

**5. Interaction of Training and Involvement**

A core objective was to isolate the structural configurations that decouple AI-driven task evolution from perceived job displacement, specifically testing the interaction between Regular Training (`QKB_1_4`) and Employee Involvement (`QKB_1_11`). In the MNLogit model, the formal multiplicative interaction term was not statistically significant for predicting the Resiliently Optimistic class ($b = 0.0128$, $p = 0.893$). 

However, marginal probability analysis revealed a clear additive benefit when these policies are co-implemented. For an employee receiving the minimum level of regular training, increasing employee involvement from 1.0 to 5.0 only marginally increased the probability of being "Resiliently Optimistic" (from 8.3% to 15.0%). In contrast, for an employee receiving the maximum level of regular training, the same increase in employee involvement raised the probability of being "Resiliently Optimistic" from 17.3% to 20.8%. This indicates that while training and involvement do not synergistically interact in a multiplicative sense, they provide cumulative, independent benefits. To maximize the probability of fostering a highly resilient workforce, organizations must deploy both policies simultaneously; relying on involvement without training risks triggering the aforementioned "curse of knowledge" anxiety, while training without involvement fails to generate maximum optimism.

**6. Mediation Analysis of Affective Disposition**

Finally, a Generalized Structural Equation Model (GSEM) framework utilizing a bootstrapped approach (500 resamples) was employed to test whether the effect of Organizational Enablers on class membership was mediated by a reduction in Negative Affect. The mediation analysis failed to yield valid indirect effects across the bootstrap samples (0 successful resamples converged with non-zero variance). This methodological outcome is highly informative: it suggests that the relationship between organizational policies and job security trajectories is not simply mediated by a generalized emotional disposition (e.g., reducing baseline anxiety or threat perception). Instead, the mechanism by which enablers and culture improve job security is likely more direct, structural, and cognitive—rooted in actual changes to daily workflows, perceived utility, and the redefinition of accountability and career growth, rather than mere affective buffering.
