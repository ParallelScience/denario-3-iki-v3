The current analysis is a significant methodological improvement over prior bivariate and OLS approaches, successfully shifting the focus from "average effects" to "workforce heterogeneity." The use of Latent Class Analysis (LCA) to identify psychological trajectories is well-justified and provides actionable insights. However, the following gaps must be addressed to ensure the robustness of the conclusions:

1. **Address the "Not Sure" Bias:** The sensitivity analysis reveals that "Not sure" respondents are not missing at random; they are systematically different (e.g., training impacts them more). Treating "Not sure" as a distinct category in the multinomial model—rather than just a sensitivity check—is necessary. If "Not sure" is a latent state of "uninformed," it should be modeled as a predictor of class transition rather than just an excluded group.

2. **Refine the "Affective Disposition" Factor:** The reported Cronbach’s alpha of 0.06 is a red flag. While you correctly identify this as an artifact of bipolar items, it suggests that the "Affective Valence" factor may be masking two distinct psychological processes: *hope* and *fear*. Instead of a single bipolar factor, re-run the EFA to extract two separate, unipolar factors (Positive Affect and Negative Affect). This will allow you to test if "hope" and "fear" have independent, asymmetric effects on job security, which is more theoretically sound than assuming they are perfect opposites.

3. **Clarify the "Company Culture" Mechanism:** You claim culture protects against the "Anxiously Declining" class, but the current model treats culture as a static index. Given that "speaking candidly" and "learning from mistakes" are components of this index, test whether this effect is moderated by "Job Level" (`QDG`). It is plausible that culture provides psychological safety for managers but not for individual contributors. This interaction is critical for actionable policy recommendations.

4. **Causal Interpretation vs. Correlation:** The claim that "involvement... increases the odds" is a strong causal assertion. Given the cross-sectional nature of the data, acknowledge the possibility of reverse causality: employees who are already "Resiliently Optimistic" may be more likely to seek out or perceive opportunities for involvement. To strengthen this, perform a "placebo test" by regressing class membership on a variable that should *not* logically influence job security (e.g., `Global Employee Size` or `Market Capitalization`). If these show significant effects, your model is picking up organizational scale bias rather than policy impact.

5. **Actionable Insight for Future Iterations:** The attenuation of training effects in the complete-case analysis is your most valuable finding. Future research should pivot from "Does training work?" to "For whom does training work?" I recommend a formal moderation analysis in the next iteration: `Class ~ Training * (Baseline_Uncertainty)`. This will provide a precise, evidence-based recommendation for HR leaders on how to segment their training interventions.

6. **Simplify Reporting:** The marginal effects analysis is excellent. Keep this as the primary communication tool for the paper, as it is far more interpretable for a non-technical audience than the multinomial logit coefficients.
