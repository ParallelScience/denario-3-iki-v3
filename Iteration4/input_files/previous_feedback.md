The current analysis provides a sophisticated psychological framing of AI-induced job security, successfully moving beyond simple bivariate correlations. However, the study suffers from significant methodological gaps that undermine its causal claims and practical utility.

**1. Critical Methodological Weaknesses:**
*   **The "Resilience" Selection Bias:** By filtering the sample to only "Resiliently Optimistic" and "Anxiously Declining" (N=1,059), you have discarded the "Stagnant Neutral" class (likely the largest segment). This is a major missed opportunity. The "Stagnant Neutral" group represents the "wait-and-see" workforce; understanding why they remain neutral—and what triggers their transition into anxiety or optimism—is more valuable for organizational policy than comparing two polarized extremes.
*   **Endogeneity of "Affective Disposition":** You treat Affective Disposition as a predictor of job security. However, it is highly probable that perceived job security *causes* the affective state (reverse causality). The current model assumes a unidirectional path that is not supported by the cross-sectional nature of the data.
*   **The "Not Sure" Exclusion:** Excluding "Not sure" respondents as MAR (Missing at Random) is a dangerous assumption. In survey research, "Not sure" is often a systematic response from employees who are disengaged or lack information. By removing them, you are likely biasing your results toward the "informed" segment of the workforce.

**2. Actionable Improvements for Future Iterations:**
*   **Re-integrate the "Stagnant Neutral" Class:** Use Multinomial Logistic Regression instead of Binary Logistic Regression. This allows you to model the probability of being "Anxious" vs. "Neutral" vs. "Resilient." This will reveal if organizational enablers (like training) act as a "buffer" (moving people from Anxious to Neutral) or a "catalyst" (moving people from Neutral to Resilient).
*   **Address Reverse Causality:** Since you cannot change the data, you must explicitly test for mediation. Use the "Company Enablers" (QKB) as instrumental variables or exogenous shocks. If training (QKB_1_4) predicts job security *independent* of the affective state, you have a stronger case for policy intervention.
*   **Refine the "Dual-Pillar" Hypothesis:** Your rejection of the interaction between training and involvement is based on a linear model. It is possible that the interaction is non-linear—e.g., training only matters if involvement is present, or vice versa. Before abandoning this, visualize the interaction using a contour plot of predicted probabilities to see if there is a "sweet spot" in the data space that the p-value missed.
*   **Focus on the "Stagnant Neutral" Transition:** The most actionable insight for a paper would be identifying the "tipping point." What differentiates a "Stagnant Neutral" employee from a "Resiliently Optimistic" one? Is it simply the frequency of AI use (QC)? If so, the "Dual-Pillar" hypothesis should be re-tested specifically on the transition from Neutral to Resilient.

**3. Strategic Guidance:**
*   **Stop over-complicating the Affective model:** The EFA is robust, but don't treat these factors as "causes." Treat them as *moderators* of how employees perceive organizational support.
*   **Shift focus to "Actionability":** The paper currently concludes that "training is good." This is trivial. The real insight lies in the sectoral differences (Energy/Life Sciences). Perform a multi-group analysis to see if the *effect size* of training differs by sector. If training is more effective in high-complexity sectors, that is a publishable, high-impact finding.
*   **Final Recommendation:** Re-run the analysis using a Multinomial framework including all three classes. This will provide a more nuanced view of the "Stagnant Neutral" group, which is the most likely target for future organizational interventions.
